History: The British East India Company

In 1600, the Queen of England responded favourably to a petition by George, Earl of Cumberland and 218 others to form the British East India Company (BEIC). For a period of fifteen years, the charter awarded the company a monopoly on English trade with all countries east of the Cape of Good Hope and west of the Straits of Magellan. Any traders there without a license from the company were liable to forfeiture of their ships and cargo (half of which would go to the Crown and half to the company), as well as imprisonment at the "royal pleasure". This privileged position granted to the BEIC clearly set them up to exploit economically all regions in India where the natives and trademen wished to sell their wares. In 1609, James I renewed the East India Company's charter for an indefinite period, with the proviso that its privileges would be annulled if trade was unprofitable for three consecutive years.

Lagaan (Tax): How It Decimated the Indian Economy

One of the ways that the British East India Company ravaged the Indian economy was the demand for lagaan (tax) in India; this tax exemplifies the economic exploitation system of land revenue that was introduced. This system, along with other colonial policies, resulted in excessive taxation, economic dependency, and the destruction of local industries, all to benefit British interests. Specifically, The East India Company, and later the British government, imposed high land taxes (lagaan) on Indian peasants, often exceeding their capacity to pay. Land revenue demands were frequently fixed and time-bound, forcing peasants to borrow money to meet them, even in years of poor harvests.

Lagaan (Tax): Benefitted British Imperialism

The East India Company's policies also aimed to promote British goods over Indian-made products, leading to the decline of traditional industries like textiles and crafts. The colonial policies eventually transformed India into a raw material supplier and a market for British goods, creating an economic dependency on Britain and creating impoverishment throughout India. Specifically, excessive taxation forced many peasants to take loans from moneylenders at exorbitant interest rates, leading to further impoverishment and land loss.
